Research Program

Phenotypic innovation, read through the genome

We ask how new ecological ways of life — a new diet, a new signal, a new social role — get written into a genome. Tropical insects give us repeated, independent natural experiments in innovation, and we build the comparative genomic tools needed to read them.

System 01 · Heliconiini

Heliconius pollen-feeding

Heliconius butterflies are the only lepidopterans known to actively collect and digest pollen, extracting amino acids that extend adult lifespan and reproductive output far beyond related nectar-feeding butterflies.

We study the genomic architecture behind this innovation: which gene families expanded or were co-opted, how regulatory regions shifted, and how convergent molecular changes track the repeated evolution of related traits across Heliconiini.

Focus: Pollen-feeding evolution Species scale: 60+ genomes Collaborators: A. Freitas, S. Montgomery
System 02 · Riodinidae

Riodinid–ant associations

Many riodinid butterfly caterpillars interact closely with ants using chemical, tactile, and vibrational signals that resemble elements of the ants’ own communication systems.

We investigate the genomic basis of these associations, including the evolution of cuticular hydrocarbons, signalling structures, and host specificity.

Focus: Chemical and acoustic signalling Collaborators: A. Freitas, L. Kaminski
System 03 · Meliponini

Stingless bee cleptobiosis

Some stingless bee lineages have abandoned foraging in favour of raiding the nests of other bee species for stored resources, a form of social parasitism known as cleptobiosis.

We study how caste determination, colony chemistry, and behavioural repertoires differ between cleptobiotic lineages and their foraging relatives.

Focus: Social parasitism and caste evolution Collaborators: M. C. Arias
Additional research directions

Convergent evolution across extreme ecological transitions

Alongside our core research systems, we investigate independent evolutionary origins of unusual phenotypes and adaptations through collaborative comparative-genomic projects.

Convergent social evolution

Convergent evolution of repletism in ants

Replete workers act as living food-storage vessels, retaining large quantities of liquid nutrients in their distended gasters and sustaining colonies during periods of scarcity.

Repletism has evolved independently in multiple ant lineages. We investigate whether these repeated origins involve similar genomic, regulatory, physiological, and developmental mechanisms.

Focus Social physiology Approach Comparative genomics
Convergent environmental adaptation

Convergent adaptation to Antarctica in springtails

Antarctic springtails survive persistent cold, desiccation, seasonal darkness, limited resources, and highly variable microclimatic conditions.

We compare independently adapted Antarctic lineages to identify repeated genomic and physiological responses associated with life in one of Earth’s most extreme terrestrial environments.

Focus Extreme-environment adaptation Approach Comparative genomics
Infrastructure

Methods built for genomes without a manual

Genome assembly

Long-read comparative genomics

Reference-quality assemblies and whole-genome alignments across non-model insect clades with no prior genomic resources.

Orthology & phylogenomics

Ortholog rescue & gene-tree inference

BUSCO-based pipelines with paralogy filtering, C-family mixture models in IQ-TREE, and topology testing across competing phylogenetic hypotheses.

Single-cell & EcoEvoDevo

Linking genome to phenotype

Single-cell transcriptomics and developmental biology connect genomic signatures of selection to the traits they actually build.
