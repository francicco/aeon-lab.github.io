How does the genome build a new way of living?

The Cicconardi Lab studies the genomic basis of phenotypic innovation in tropical insects — pollen-feeding butterflies, ant-mimicking caterpillars, and social bees that live inside other species' nests. We work across comparative genomics, EcoEvoDevo, and single-cell transcriptomics.

Research Systems

Three natural experiments in phenotypic novelty

System 01

Heliconius pollen-feeding

The only butterflies known to digest pollen for amino acids — a dietary innovation layered onto a genome shaped by adaptive radiation and mimicry.

System 02

Riodinid–ant associations

Metalmark caterpillars that chemically and acoustically mimic ants to secure protection — a case study in convergent signalling across kingdoms.

System 03

Stingless bee cleptobiosis

Bees that live by raiding the nests of other bees — social parasitism as a lens on caste, behaviour, and genome evolution.

Approach

Comparative genomics, built for non-model species

Most of what we study has no reference genome, no annotated pathways, and no established husbandry protocol. We build the genomic and computational infrastructure — from long-read assembly to ortholog rescue across dozens of species — needed to ask evolutionary questions in systems that were previously out of reach.

Alongside comparative genomics, the lab runs a live insectary rearing Heliconius on Passiflora host plants, connecting genomic hypotheses to real developmental and behavioural biology.

Lab Notebook — Field Note 003

Insectary, USP campus

Rearing colonies established on Passiflora suberosa, P. misera, and P. edulis, sourced with collaborators at UFRGS. Live colonies link comparative genomic findings to observable phenotype — diet, development, and behaviour — inside the same research program.
